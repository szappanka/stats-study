1. (3 points) A dataset contains the following variables: student ID number, exam score in
points, satisfaction rating from 1 to 5, and room temperature measured in degrees Celsius.
For each variable, identify the level of measurement or variable type. Then choose one
variable for which taking an arithmetic mean would be questionable, and briefly explain
why.
Solution.
Student ID number is a nominal identifier: the numbers are labels, not quantities.
Exam score in points is quantitative; in most exam contexts it is treated as a
ratio-type or at least interval-type numerical variable, because differences in points
have meaning and zero points is a meaningful reference. Satisfaction rating from 1 to
5 is ordinal: the order is meaningful, but the distances between neighbouring
categories need not be equal. Celsius temperature is interval-scale: differences are
meaningful, but the zero point is conventional, so ratios such as “20 degrees is twice
as warm as 10 degrees” are not meaningful in Celsius.
Taking the mean of student ID numbers is meaningless, because the numerical codes do
not measure an amount. Taking the mean of ordinal satisfaction ratings is also
questionable: it is often done as a rough summary, but it assumes equal spacing
between categories, which may not be justified.

[18.2,21.6] minutes.” Decide which interpretations are correct and correct the wrong ones.

(a) There is a 95% probability that this particular interval contains the true mean.
(b) If we repeated the same sampling procedure many times, about 95% of the intervals
constructed in this way would contain the true mean.
(c) About 95% of individual waiting times are between 18.2 and 21.6 minutes.

Solution.
(a) is not the standard frequentist interpretation. After the data have been observed,
this particular interval is fixed, and the unknown mean is treated as fixed. The 95%

this already computed interval.
(b) is correct. If the assumptions are satisfied and the same method is repeated many
times, approximately 95% of the intervals produced by the procedure contain the true
mean.
(c) is wrong. A confidence interval for the mean is not an interval that should
contain 95% of the individual observations. Individual waiting times are usually much
more variable than the sample mean.
(d) is correct. The interval should be read as a range of plausible values for the
distributional mean µ, given the data and the chosen confidence level.
4. (3 points) In a test of
H0 : µ= 100 against H1 : µ̸= 100,
the computed p-value is 0.032. What is the decision at significance level α= 0.05? What
is the decision at α= 0.01? Explain why the sentence “the probability that H0 is true is
3.2%” is not a correct interpretation of the p-value.
Solution.
At α= 0.05, we reject H0, because p= 0.032 <0.05. At α= 0.01, we do not reject H0,
because p= 0.032 >0.01.
The p-value is computed under the assumption that H0 is true. It is the probability,
assuming H0, of obtaining a test statistic at least as extreme as the one observed.
It is not the probability that H0 itself is true. The incorrect sentence reverses the
conditional probability: it treats P(data or more extreme |H0) as if it were
P(H0 |data).
5. (3 points) Suppose a null hypothesis is rejected at significance level α= 0.05. What can
we conclude about the decision at α= 0.10? What can we conclude about the decision at
α= 0.01? Give a short explanation using the relation between the p-value and α.
Solution.
If H0 is rejected at α= 0.05, then the p-value must satisfy p≤0.05. Therefore it is
certainly also true that p≤0.10, so H0 would also be rejected at α= 0.10.
At α= 0.01, we cannot know the decision from this information alone. If, for example,
p= 0.008, then we reject at 0.01. If p= 0.03, then we reject at 0.05 but not at 0.01.
A smaller significance level means a stricter rejection rule.

6. (3 points) A smoke alarm is treated as a hypothesis test. The null hypothesis is “there is
no dangerous smoke”, and the alarm rings when the null hypothesis is rejected. In this
context, describe a Type I error and a Type II error. Which error becomes more likely if
the alarm is made much less sensitive? Briefly justify your answer.
Solution.
A Type I error means rejecting a true null hypothesis. Here this means that there is
no dangerous smoke, but the alarm rings anyway. This is a false alarm.
A Type II error means not rejecting a false null hypothesis. Here this means that
there is dangerous smoke, but the alarm does not ring. This is a missed detection.
If the alarm is made much less sensitive, it rings less often. Therefore false alarms
become less likely, but missed detections become more likely. In hypothesis-testing
language, decreasing sensitivity reduces the chance of Type I errors but increases the
chance of Type II errors and decreases the power of the test.

8. (3 points) A researcher fits the simple linear regression model
Yi = β0 + β1xi + εi,
where Yi is the final exam score and xi is the number of hours studied. The estimated slope
is positive. A residual-versus-fitted plot shows a clear curved pattern rather than a random
cloud. Interpret β1 in words, explain what the curved residual pattern suggests, and decide
whether the positive slope alone proves that studying more causes higher exam scores.
Solution.
The parameter β1 is the slope parameter of the regression model. In this context it
means the expected change in final exam score associated with one additional hour
studied, according to the linear model and holding the model structure fixed. If
β1 >0, the fitted linear relationship says that larger values of x are associated with
larger expected values of Y.
A curved pattern in the residual-versus-fitted plot suggests that the linear form may
be misspecified. The relationship between studying time and exam score may be
nonlinear, or an important variable may be missing. In a well-fitting simple linear
model, residuals should not show a systematic curve as a function of the fitted values.
The positive slope alone does not prove causality. This is an observational
association unless the study design justifies causal interpretation, for example

9. (3 points) A daily time series records the number of visitors to a website. The plot shows
a long-term upward trend and a clear weekly pattern. Is weak stationarity plausible for
the original series? Name one transformation or modelling step that could make the series
closer to stationary. What would a large positive autocorrelation at lag 7 suggest?
Solution.
Weak stationarity is not very plausible for the original series. A weakly stationary
time series should have a constant expected value and an autocovariance structure
depending only on the lag, not on the actual time point. A long-term upward trend
suggests a changing mean, and a weekly pattern suggests systematic seasonality.
Possible steps include removing or modelling the trend, taking first differences,
subtracting a fitted trend, using seasonal differencing with lag 7, or explicitly
including weekday effects in a model. A large positive autocorrelation at lag 7
suggests weekly seasonality: values observed on the same day of the week tend to be
similar.
10. (2 points) A company tests 20 different website variants, each at the 5% significance level,
and then announces the one variant for which p= 0.04. What is the statistical problem
with this reasoning? Name one way to make the analysis more reliable.
Solution.
The problem is multiple testing, often connected with data snooping or p-hacking. If
many hypotheses are tested, then even when there is no real effect, the chance of
finding at least one apparently significant result can be much larger than 5%.
One solution is to adjust for multiple comparisons, for example by using a Bonferroni
correction or a false discovery rate procedure. Another solution is to pre-register
the primary comparison before looking at the data and treat the remaining comparisons
as exploratory.
